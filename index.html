What even is nuclear energy?

Google defines nuclear energy as "the energy released during nuclear fission or fusion, especially when used to generate electricity." While this is a good definition, I personally feel it gives people a false sense of security; that nuclear energy is "just like all the others". This definition, while scientific and accurate, makes it easy for curious readers to believe it is approachable and understood, rather than a colossal burden to scientists, urban planners, safety experts, architects, and many more.

Currently, according to the World Nuclear Association, over 50 countries have nuclear power on 5 continents (the two that don't are Oceania and Antarctica, if you're wondering). 9 countries have nuclear weapons, meaning that out of almost 200 nations, only 9 control the fate of mankindâ€”some of whom are simply regional powers. Nuclear energy, in basic terms, works in two different ways: fission and fusion. Fission involves radioactive atoms, such as uranium-235 and plutonium, splitting their nucleus apart. The particles, usually neutrons, coming off creates a huge amount of energy. Then, the material is placed into fuel rods. These rods get incredibly hot. Some are almost half the temperature of the Sun. (Remember, these are paid for by your tax dollars and put within view of your backyard.) The rods are put into water to cool them down, causing a large amount of steam which is then turned en masse and creates clean energy.

Fusion is far more uncommon; two nuclei in two atoms are "fused" together to form a bigger nucleus. The energy that is released from this fusion is then turned into heat and energy which is harvested and used in the reaction. Nuclear fusion is far harder to achieve and far more expensive; however, it releases more energy on average than fission.

There are many reasons to be against the use of nuclear energy, as further discussed on additional pages.
